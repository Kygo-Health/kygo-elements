## General Information

VO2 max is the single best lab measure of cardiorespiratory fitness and one of the strongest predictors of all-cause mortality. It is what your watch estimates when it shows a "fitness age" or cardio score. This guide breaks the factors that move VO2 max into categories: exercise/training, nutrition and supplements, environment, demographics and physiology, lifestyle, and clinical conditions. A separate section covers how consumer wearables estimate VO2 max and how accurate they are versus a lab test. As with the other library files, each row has a plain-English column and an evidence grade, and (with one noted exception) every claim is anchored to a primary source.

A note on what VO2 max actually responds to: training and biology dominate. Most supplements that get marketed for "endurance" do not raise VO2 max itself, they change the oxygen cost of a given effort (economy). We call that out explicitly below because it is a common misconception.

## Definitions

- **VO2 max:** The maximum rate your body can take in, transport, and use oxygen during all-out exercise. Usually expressed in mL/kg/min.
- **VO2 peak:** The highest VO2 actually reached in a test when a true plateau is not confirmed. Often used interchangeably with VO2 max in studies.
- **Oxygen cost / economy:** How much oxygen you burn at a fixed submaximal effort. Better economy means less O2 for the same work. This is separate from VO2 max.
- **Trainability:** How much your VO2 max improves in response to a training program. Varies hugely between people.
- **SMD:** Standardized mean difference, an effect size. Roughly: 0.2 small, 0.5 moderate, 0.8 large.
- **MICT:** Moderate-intensity continuous training (steady cardio). **HIIT:** High-intensity interval training.

Great low-time option, but they are genuinely unpleasant. |
| Resistance training (alone) | Positive (modest) | Moderate | Meta-analysis in healthy older adults (22 studies): resistance training alone improved VO2 peak (about +1.9 mL/kg/min), but less than aerobic or interval training. | Lifting does give a modest VO2 max bump, especially if you are deconditioned, but cardio and intervals raise it more. Best used alongside, not instead of, aerobic work. |
| Detraining (stopping training) | Negative | Strong | Meta of 21 athlete studies: VO2 max fell after short-term (<30 days) cessation (ES -0.62, avg -3.93%) and more after long-term (30 to 90 days) cessation (ES -1.42, avg -9.43%). Higher-trained athletes lose more. | Fitness is use-it-or-lose-it. You start losing VO2 max within ~2 weeks of stopping, and the fitter you are the more you have to lose. ## Nutrition & Supplements

| Factor | Direction | Evidence | Key Finding | Plain English |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Iron (when deficient) | Positive | Strong | Meta of 24 RCTs in women of reproductive age: daily iron raised relative VO2 max by 2.35 mL/kg/min (95% CI 0.82 to 3.88, p=0.003) and lowered submaximal heart rate. | Fixing low iron raises VO2 max, especially in menstruating women who are prone to deficiency. This is correcting a deficit, not a boost on top of normal iron. |
| Dietary nitrate / beetroot juice | No effect on VO2 max | Strong | 73 studies (n=1061): nitrate lowered submaximal VO2 (O2 cost) by ~0.04 L/min (p<0.00001) but had no significant effect on VO2 max. | Beetroot does not raise your ceiling. It makes a given pace cheaper to hold (better economy), which can help performance without changing VO2 max. |
| Caffeine | No reliable effect on VO2 max | Moderate | RCT (n=9): caffeine improved time to exhaustion (~+19%) and peak power (~+13%) but did not change VO2 max, and the gains were matched by a placebo believed to be caffeine. Broader reviews agree caffeine is ergogenic without raising VO2 max. | Caffeine helps you push harder and feel less effort, which improves performance. It does not raise your VO2 max ceiling. A lot of the benefit is expectation/placebo. |
| Creatine | Slightly negative (relative VO2 max) | Strong | Meta of 19 RCTs (n=424): VO2 max rose less with creatine than placebo (ES -0.32, 95% CI -0.51 to -0.12). | Creatine is great for strength and power, but it adds water weight, and since VO2 max is measured per kg, the number can dip slightly. It does not improve aerobic capacity. |
| Beta-alanine | No effect on VO2 max | Moderate | ISSN position stand: beta-alanine raises muscle carnosine and helps efforts lasting ~30 s to 10 min, but does not increase VO2 max. | Useful for buffering "the burn" in short hard efforts. It does not raise your VO2 max ceiling. |
| Sodium bicarbonate | No effect on VO2 max | Moderate | Systematic review: bicarbonate buffers exercise acidosis and aids high-intensity efforts (especially >4 min), but is not shown to raise VO2 max, and it does not change the VO2 slow component. | "Baking soda" loading helps you tolerate the burn in hard efforts. It does not raise your aerobic ceiling. |
| Vitamin D | No consistent effect on VO2 max | Moderate | Network meta-analysis of 6 supplements (30 RCTs, 693 athletes): none, including vitamin D, significantly raised VO2 max. Individual deficiency-correction studies are mixed. | Worth keeping vitamin D in normal range for general health, but supplementing it does not reliably raise VO2 max unless you were deficient. |
| Omega-3 (fish oil) | No consistent effect on VO2 max | Moderate | ISSN position stand: omega-3 (EPA/DHA) chiefly lowers the oxygen cost of submaximal work and reduces exercising heart rate; evidence for raising VO2 max itself is inconsistent (some small VO2 peak gains, many null). | Fish oil may make a given effort feel a touch easier and is good for general health, but it is not a reliable way to raise your VO2 max ceiling. |

*Anchors: Pasricha et al. 2014 meta (iron); Gao/Whitlock et al. 2021 meta (nitrate); Brietzke et al. 2017 RCT (caffeine); Gras et al. 2023 meta (creatine); Trexler et al. 2015 ISSN position stand (beta-alanine); Hadzic et al. 2019 review (bicarbonate); Deng et al. 2025 network meta (vitamin D); ISSN omega-3 position stand 2024 (omega-3).*

## Environment

| Factor | Direction | Evidence | Key Finding | Plain English |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Altitude (live high, train low) | Positive | Moderate | Elite orienteers living at 2,500 m (18 h/day) and training lower for 24 days raised hemoglobin mass (805 to 848 g) and VO2 max (3,515 to 3,660 mL/min); sea-level controls did not change. | Sleeping at altitude builds more oxygen-carrying red cells, which can lift VO2 max. Evidence is from small elite-athlete studies and the effect is still debated. |
| Acute altitude (being at altitude) | Negative | Strong | VO2 max drops ~6 to 8% per 1,000 m above sea level in elite athletes; about half recovers with ~2 weeks of acclimatization. | The thinner the air, the lower your VO2 max on the spot. Your watch will read lower at elevation until you adapt. |
| Heat acclimation | Positive | Moderate | Meta-analysis: repeated heat exposure raises VO2 max by a small to moderate-large amount (larger when tested in the heat). A key trial showed +5% in cool and +8% in hot conditions, via ~6.5% plasma-volume expansion. | Training in the heat expands blood plasma and can nudge VO2 max up, similar in spirit to altitude. The boost is clearest when you are also tested in heat; transfer to cool-weather performance is less certain. |
| Air pollution (PM2.5) | Negative | Moderate | Systematic review: breathing polluted air during endurance exercise impairs cardiopulmonary responses and can blunt the aerobic-fitness benefit of training; fine particulates (PM2.5) reach the lungs and bloodstream. | Hard training in heavy traffic or smog works against you. The dirtier the air, the more it eats into the aerobic gains you are trying to build. Favor cleaner air and times of day when you can. |
| Hypoxic training methods | Positive (varies by method) | Moderate | Network meta-analysis (59 studies): live-high/train-low, live-high/train-high, and intermittent hypoxic training all beat normoxic training for VO2 max, with live-high/train-low plus low-altitude training ranking best. Plain aerobic intermittent hypoxic training alone is the least reliable. | Structured altitude/hypoxia protocols can raise VO2 max, but the method matters: sleeping high while training low is the most dependable. Just doing intervals in a hypoxic mask is the weakest version. |

*Anchors: Wehrlin et al. 2006 (live high, train low); Waldron et al. 2021 meta + Lorenzo et al. 2010 (heat acclimation); González-Rojas et al. 2025 systematic review (air pollution); Feng et al. 2023 network meta (hypoxic training methods). Note: the "acute altitude ~6 to 8% per 1,000 m" row is a well-established textbook figure from the broader altitude-physiology literature surfaced during research, not a single numbered source above; flag for a dedicated citation in a future pass.*

## Demographics & Physiology

| Factor | Direction | Evidence | Key Finding | Plain English |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Body fat / excess weight | Negative (relative VO2 max) | Strong | VO2 max is usually expressed per kg of body weight, so carrying more fat lowers the number. In adults with overweight/obesity, training raised relative VO2 max by 3.82 mL/kg/min (66 RCTs, n=3,964). | Because the score is "per kilo," losing fat raises your VO2 max number even if your heart and lungs do not change. Training plus fat loss is the big lever here. |
| Age | Negative | Strong | In the Baltimore Longitudinal Study (n=810, ages 21 to 87), peak VO2 decline accelerates each decade rather than being linear, and is faster in men. Cross-sectional studies underestimate the true rate. | VO2 max drops with age, and the drop speeds up the older you get. Single snapshots across ages make aging look gentler than it really is. |
| Immobilization / bed rest | Negative (severe) | Strong | Dallas Bed Rest Study (5 healthy men): 3 weeks of strict bed rest cut VO2 max ~27%, reversed by 8 weeks of training. A 40-year follow-up found that 40 years of aging caused about the same proportional drop as those 3 weeks of bed rest. | Extended inactivity is brutally fast at erasing fitness. Three weeks flat on your back cost these men as much VO2 max as four decades of aging. |
| Blood donation (acute) | Negative (temporary) | Moderate | Systematic review found a mean VO2 max reduction after ~500 mL whole-blood donation (medium effect size, Cohen's d), driven by reduced cardiac output and oxygen-carrying capacity; individual studies report roughly a 4 to 11% acute drop, recovering over ~2 to 3 weeks as red cells rebuild. | Donating blood temporarily dents your VO2 max because you have fewer red cells to carry oxygen. It bounces back in a couple of weeks. Time donations away from key events. |
| Dehydration / hypohydration | Negative | Strong | Losing more than ~2% of body mass as water lowers maximal aerobic capacity, mainly by shrinking plasma volume, which reduces stroke volume and cardiac output. The impairment compounds in the heat (high skin temperature). | Showing up dehydrated lowers your VO2 max on the day because there is less blood volume to pump. Hot conditions make it worse. Hydrate before testing or hard efforts. |
| Genetics (trainability) | Variable | Strong | HERITAGE Family Study (n=481, 98 families, 20 wk identical training): mean gain ~400 mL/min but ranged from no gain to >1.0 L/min. ~47% of the response was heritable; 2.5x more variance between families than within. | How much your VO2 max improves from the same training is roughly half genetic. Some people are high responders, some barely move. It runs in families. |
| Sex | Variable | Moderate (narrative review) | ~10 to 12% endurance performance gap. Even adjusted per kg or per fat-free mass, men average ~5 to 10% higher VO2 max, driven mainly by greater oxygen delivery (higher hemoglobin mass, larger heart). | Men average higher VO2 max largely because they carry more oxygen per unit of blood and have bigger hearts. Part of the gap remains even after correcting for body size and muscle. |

*Anchors: Fleg et al. 2005 (age); Bouchard et al. 1999 HERITAGE (genetics); Santisteban et al. 2022 (sex); Saltin et al. 1968 Dallas Bed Rest (immobilization); van Baak et al. 2021 (body fat); Johnson et al. 2019 (blood donation); Cheuvront/Sawka 2010 (dehydration).*

## Lifestyle

| Factor | Direction | Evidence | Key Finding | Plain English |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Smoking | Negative | Moderate | Current smokers had lower estimated VO2 max (38.8 mL/kg/min) than never-smokers (41.6) and former smokers (41.4); former and never smokers did not differ. Carbon monoxide binds hemoglobin ~200x harder than oxygen, cutting oxygen delivery. | Smoking lowers your aerobic ceiling, mostly by hogging the oxygen seats on your red blood cells. The good news: quitters in this study matched never-smokers, so it appears reversible. |
| Alcohol (previous-day) | Negative (performance) | Moderate | RCT (n=12): a previous-evening alcohol dose cut next-morning cycling time to exhaustion by ~11% (181 vs 203 s); strength tests were unaffected. Effect is on endurance performance/capacity rather than a proven change in VO2 max itself. | Drinking the night before a hard session measurably hurts endurance the next day. The hit is to how long you can hold an effort, more than to your VO2 max ceiling. |
| Sleep deprivation | Negative (performance) | Moderate | Meta-analysis (45 studies): sleep deprivation impairs aerobic endurance performance and raises perceived exertion. Acute sleep loss does not reliably change measured VO2 max itself, but chronic poor sleep is linked to lower VO2 max and blunts training gains. | A bad night mostly hurts how long and how hard you can go, not your peak VO2 number on a single test. But consistently short sleep undercuts the fitness you build over time. |
| Sauna bathing (habitual) | Positive | Moderate | Kuopio cohort plus a small RCT: regular sauna use is associated with higher cardiorespiratory fitness, and adding post-exercise sauna to training improved VO2 by ~2.7 mL/kg/min versus exercise alone. | Sauna triggers heart-rate and plasma-volume responses a bit like exercise, and stacking it onto training may give a small extra VO2 max bump. Evidence is mostly observational plus one small trial. |
| Sedentary behavior (sitting time) | Negative | Moderate | In 16,025 adults, more daily sitting was associated with lower cardiorespiratory fitness, independent of exercise, with the strongest link in otherwise inactive people. | Long sitting days drag down your fitness even if you also work out. Breaking up sitting matters on top of your training, not instead of it. |
| Cold-water immersion (post-training) | Neutral for VO2 max | Moderate | Meta-analysis: routinely ice-bathing after sessions blunts strength and power gains (SMD -0.60) but does not compromise endurance/aerobic adaptations; it can stay in an endurance athlete's recovery toolkit. | Ice baths mainly cost you strength gains, not VO2 max. If you are training for endurance, regular cold plunges appear fine; if you are chasing strength, use them sparingly. |

*Anchors: Caci et al. 2024 (smoking; step-test estimated VO2 max, small sample); Levitt et al. 2022 (alcohol; n=12, time-to-exhaustion not VO2 max); Kong et al. 2025 meta (sleep deprivation); Kunutsor et al. 2024 (sauna; observational + small RCT); Eriksen et al. 2016 (sedentary behavior; observational, n=16,025); Malta et al. 2021 meta (cold-water immersion).*

## Clinical

| Factor | Direction | Evidence | Key Finding | Plain English |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Long COVID | Negative | Strong | Meta-analysis (9 studies, 464 symptomatic vs 359): peak VO2 was 4.9 mL/kg/min lower in people with ongoing symptoms 3+ months after infection. Drivers include deconditioning, dysfunctional breathing, chronotropic incompetence, and impaired oxygen extraction. | Lingering COVID symptoms come with a measurably lower VO2 max, comparable to deconditioning from bed rest. Some is reversible with graded reconditioning, but mechanisms vary by person. |
| Type 2 diabetes | Negative | Strong | Meta-analysis (77 studies): people with type 2 diabetes averaged 5.84 mL/kg/min lower VO2 max than those without (roughly 20% lower). Driven by cardiac, microvascular, and mitochondrial impairments. | Type 2 diabetes meaningfully lowers aerobic fitness, and low fitness in turn predicts worse outcomes. Exercise that raises VO2 max is one of the highest-value interventions here. |
| Heart failure (and VO2 max as prognosis) | Negative | Strong | Heart failure lowers peak VO2, and the value itself is prognostic: in the landmark CPET study, peak VO2 ≤14 mL/kg/min marked the worst-prognosis group and became a heart-transplant timing threshold. | The heart can't deliver enough oxygen, so VO2 max falls and is one of the strongest predictors of survival in heart failure. It is used to time transplant decisions. |
| Low VO2 max / fitness as a mortality risk factor | Predictor (higher = lower mortality) | Strong | In 122,007 adults followed ~8.4 years, higher cardiorespiratory fitness was associated with lower all-cause mortality with no upper limit of benefit; the least-fit had ~5x the adjusted mortality risk of the fittest — a risk comparable to or greater than smoking, diabetes, or coronary disease. | The "why it matters" headline: low VO2 max is one of the strongest predictors of dying early, on par with major disease risk factors — and there's no point of diminishing returns. Raising it is among the highest-leverage things you can do for longevity. |

*Anchors: Durstenfeld et al. 2022 (long COVID); Macedo et al. 2023 meta (type 2 diabetes); Mancini et al. 1991 (heart failure prognosis / transplant threshold); Mandsager et al. 2018 (CRF and all-cause mortality, n=122,007).*

## Wearable VO2 Max Estimation: Methods & Accuracy

Important framing for Kygo's audience: consumer wearables do not *measure* VO2 max. The lab gold standard (CPET / indirect calorimetry) analyzes the oxygen and carbon dioxide in your breath during a maximal test. Watches and straps have no gas analysis, so they *estimate* VO2 max from heart rate, motion, GPS pace, and your profile (age, sex, weight). Treat the number as a fitness trend tracker, not a clinical value. This section complements files #06 (Wearable Accuracy) and #07 (Sensor Comparison).

The single most useful accuracy finding comes from the INTERLIVE meta-analysis of consumer wearables: **how the device collects the data matters more than the brand.** Wearables that estimate VO2 max from an actual workout (exercise-based) had near-zero average bias vs lab (−0.09 mL/kg/min), while those that estimate it at rest (resting-based) overestimated by +2.17 mL/kg/min. Both, however, have large *individual* error (limits of agreement roughly ±13 to 17 mL/kg/min for resting methods), which is why the number is a trend/population tool, not a precise personal value.

Two estimation approaches:

- **Exercise-based HR-to-pace modeling (more accurate).** During an outdoor walk/run the device models the heart-rate-to-pace relationship and extrapolates to your max. Used by Garmin (Firstbeat), Apple (outdoor walk/run/hike), Polar (running-based test), Samsung (outdoor run), Coros/Suunto/Huawei (similar HR-to-pace), and WHOOP's GPS model. Needs a real cardio effort, a clean HR signal, and a correct max-HR assumption.
- **Resting / non-exercise estimation (least accurate, tends to overestimate).** From resting heart rate plus age/sex/weight, no workout needed. Used by Polar's Fitness Test (resting HR + HRV), Fitbit's cardio fitness score without GPS, and WHOOP's passive model. Oura's Cardio Capacity sits in between: a guided in-app 6-minute walk test.

The table below separates what each company *claims* from what *independent* (non-vendor) studies found vs lab CPET. Pattern: vendors quote best-case numbers; independent studies are a bit worse and consistently show errors balloon in highly trained athletes and with wrist-only optical HR.

**Verification note (triple-checked).** Independent (non-vendor) peer-reviewed VO2 max validation exists only for Garmin (Carrier 2025; Engel 2025; plus the Garmin-heavy Železnik Mežan 2025 review and INTERLIVE meta), Apple (Lambe 2025; Caserman 2024), Polar (Neudorfer 2025), and Fitbit (Freeberg 2019). For Samsung, WHOOP, Oura, and Coros, repeated targeted searches found no independent VO2 max validation — only vendor or company-funded claims. A frequently-repeated "Frontiers in Physiology, Galaxy Watch 5/6, 84 participants, ±4.7 mL/kg/min" VO2 max figure appears only on AI-content/marketing sites and could not be traced to a real indexed study (PubMed/PMC/journal); it is deliberately excluded. If any of these brands publish independent validation later, add it here.

Bottom line: a VO2 max estimate from a hard outdoor run with a chest strap (Garmin/Firstbeat, the only brand with solid independent validation — ~5 to 7% error in general populations) is the most trustworthy. Apple has two independent studies and both show it *underestimates* (~13 to 16%). Polar's resting test and Fitbit overestimate. Samsung, WHOOP, Oura, and Coros rely on company claims with little or no independent peer-reviewed VO2 max validation. For every device, accuracy degrades in highly trained people (errors of 9 to 16%), with wrist-only optical HR, in heat, and when the assumed max HR is wrong. Use the number to track *your own trend over time*, not to compare people or hit a clinical threshold.

*Anchors (independent): Molina-García et al. 2022 INTERLIVE meta (exercise- vs resting-based); Carrier et al. 2025 + Engel et al. 2025 + Železnik Mežan 2025 (Garmin); Lambe et al. 2025 + Caserman et al. 2024 (Apple); Neudorfer et al. 2025 (Polar); Freeberg et al. 2019 (Fitbit). Vendor/company-associated claims (Firstbeat, Samsung/U. Michigan, WHOOP, Oura, Coros) are labeled as such and not independently verified.*

Rings/straps and resting-based watches are convenient but either overestimate, lack independent validation, or sit behind a subscription. For tracking *change in your own fitness*, any of them works if you keep the device and protocol consistent; for an accurate *absolute* number, only a lab CPET qualifies.

### What Each Device Measures (Inputs) and How Good Those Inputs Are

VO2 max is only as accurate as the signals fed into it. None of these devices measure oxygen; they infer VO2 max from heart rate, GPS pace, and your profile. Table A shows what goes in; Table B shows how well-validated each input is independently. The punchline: the limiting input is heart rate *during the run* plus the assumed maximum HR, which is exactly why a chest strap helps and why very fit people get poor readings.

| Garmin (watch) | Wrist optical or paired chest strap | Yes | No | No | Yes | Yes |
| Apple Watch | Wrist optical | Yes | No | No | Yes | Yes |
| Polar (Fitness Test) | Wrist optical / chest, at rest | No (resting); Yes in run-test mode | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Fitbit / Google | Wrist optical | Optional (a GPS run refines it) | No | Yes (core input) | Yes | Yes |

**Table B — Independent accuracy of each input signal**

| Input signal | How good it is (independent evidence) | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Chest-strap HR (Polar H10) | Effectively ECG-grade at rest and during exercise (RR/HR agreement >99%) | Gilgen-Ammann 2019; #06 (r=0.99 vs ECG) |
| Wrist optical HR, steady effort | Generally accurate; 6 of 7 wrist devices tracked HR within ~5% of ECG (though none measured energy expenditure well: 27-93% off) | Shcherbina 2017 |

| GPS pace/distance | MAPE 3.2-6.1% across 8 watches; only Polar <5%; worse in urban/forest, better on track | 8-watch GPS study 2020 |

**Reconciliation with #06/#07 (checked).** The heart-rate and sensor figures above were sourced fresh and then cross-checked against file #06 (Wearable Accuracy). They agree: #06 independently lists chest strap r=0.99, Apple active-HR r=0.80, Garmin r=0.52 (Dial et al. 2025), Oura resting-HR CCC 0.98, and — importantly — the same VO2 max figures used here (Garmin Fēnix 6 MAPE 7.05%, Apple 13-16%). No contradictions were found between this section and #06/#07. The one caveat carried over from the library's Oura file: Oura's headline HR/HRV validation (Kinnunen 2020) is Oura-employee-authored; independent work (Cao 2022, Dial 2025) confirms strong *resting* accuracy but Oura has no active-exercise HR.

Why this matters for VO2 max: a device can have a good HR sensor and still produce a shaky VO2 max, because the estimate leans on HR *during hard effort* (where wrist optical drifts) and on an assumed max HR (often just 220−age). That combination — not the sensor at rest — is the real ceiling on accuracy, and it explains why chest-strap + outdoor run (Garmin) is the most trustworthy setup and why highly trained users see the biggest errors.

   - Design: systematic review + meta-analysis, 22 studies (VO2peak) in healthy older adults. Verified claim: resistance training alone improved VO2peak (~+1.9 mL/kg/min, larger in interventions <24 weeks), but aerobic/interval training raises VO2max more. Authors/journal/DOI confirmed.

28. **Environment / air pollution.** González-Rojas S, Yáñez-Sepúlveda R, Tuesta M, Sánchez-Ureña B, Trejos-Montoya J, Olivares-Arancibia J, López-Gil JF, Rojas-Valverde D. "Air Pollution and Endurance Exercise: A Systematic Review of the Potential Effects on Cardiopulmonary Health." *Life.* 2025;15(4):595. PMID 40283151. DOI [10.3390/life15040595](https://doi.org/10.3390/life15040595).
   - Design: systematic review (20-year window; PubMed/Elsevier/WoS). Verified claim: exposure to air pollution during endurance activity (cycling, outdoor running) significantly affects cardiopulmonary health; PM2.5 penetrates lungs and bloodstream. Used to support the direction (pollution impairs aerobic responses / blunts training benefit); specific magnitudes vary by study. Authors/journal/DOI confirmed.

29. **Lifestyle / sleep deprivation.** Kong Y, Yu B, Guan G, Wang Y, He H. "Effects of sleep deprivation on sports performance and perceived exertion in athletes and non-athletes: a systematic review and meta-analysis." *Frontiers in Physiology.* 2025. PMCID PMC11996801. DOI [10.3389/fphys.2025.1544286](https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2025.1544286).
   - Design: systematic review + meta-analysis, 45 studies (16 on aerobic endurance). Verified claim: sleep deprivation significantly impairs aerobic endurance performance and increases RPE. Paired with the established finding that acute sleep loss does not reliably change measured VO2max itself (the row makes this distinction explicit). Authors/journal/DOI confirmed.

30. **Lifestyle / sauna.** Kunutsor SK, Isiozor NM, Kurl S, Laukkanen JA. "Enhancing Cardiorespiratory Fitness Through Sauna Bathing: Insights From the Kuopio Ischemic Heart Disease Prospective Study." *Journal of Cardiopulmonary Rehabilitation and Prevention.* 2024;44(4):295-300. PMID [38836690](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38836690/).
   - Design: prospective cohort (Kuopio, 2,291 men) plus discussion of a small RCT. Verified figures: habitual sauna use associated with higher cardiorespiratory fitness; in a referenced RCT, exercise + post-exercise sauna improved CRF by +2.7 mL/kg/min (95% CI +0.2 to +5.3) vs exercise alone. Evidence is mostly observational plus one small trial; framed as moderate. Authors/journal/pages confirmed.

### Batch 7 (added 2026-06-02) — Exercise / Environment / Lifestyle only

31. **Exercise / tapering.** Bosquet L, Montpetit J, Arvisais D, Mujika I. "Effects of tapering on performance: a meta-analysis." *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise.* 2007;39(8):1358-1365. PMID 17762369. DOI [10.1249/mss.0b013e31806010e0](https://doi.org/10.1249/mss.0b013e31806010e0).
   - Design: meta-analysis, 27 of 182 studies met criteria. Verified claim: a 2-week taper with training volume exponentially reduced 41 to 60% (intensity and frequency maintained) maximizes performance; VO2max is maintained or modestly improved as fatigue dissipates. Authors/journal/pages/PMID confirmed.

32. **Exercise / concurrent training.** Gao J, Yu L. "Effects of concurrent training sequence on VO2max and lower limb strength performance: A systematic review and meta-analysis." *Frontiers in Physiology.* 2023;14:1072679. DOI [10.3389/fphys.2023.1072679](https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2023.1072679). (Corrigendum 10.3389/fphys.2023.1192593, funding statement only.)
   - Design: systematic review + meta-analysis of concurrent (strength+endurance) training sequences. Verified claim: concurrent training improves VO2max; sequence (strength-first vs endurance-first) affects lower-limb strength more than VO2max, i.e. adding strength work does not meaningfully blunt aerobic gains. Used to support "neutral for VO2max"; broader interference literature agrees the interference effect falls mainly on strength/hypertrophy (some VO2max blunting possible in untrained). Authors/journal/DOI confirmed.

33. **Environment / hypoxic training.** Feng X, Zhao L, Chen Y, Wang Z, Lu H, Wang C. "Optimal type and dose of hypoxic training for improving maximal aerobic capacity in athletes: a systematic review and Bayesian model-based network meta-analysis." *Frontiers in Physiology.* 2023;14:1223037. PMID 37745240. DOI [10.3389/fphys.2023.1223037](https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2023.1223037).
   - Design: systematic review + Bayesian network meta-analysis, 59 studies (LHTH, LHTL, HHL, IHT, IHE). Verified claim: LHTL, LHTH, and IHT all outperformed normoxic training for VO2max; LHTL combined with low-altitude training ranked best (P-scores ~0.86-0.92). Complemented by a 2025 meta finding plain aerobic IHT is not reliably beneficial. Authors/journal/PMID/DOI confirmed.

34. **Lifestyle / sedentary behavior.** Eriksen L, Grønbæk M, Helge JW, Tolstrup JS. "Cardiorespiratory fitness in 16 025 adults aged 18-91 years and associations with physical activity and sitting time." *Scandinavian Journal of Medicine & Science in Sports.* 2016;26(12):1435-1443. PMID 26681406. DOI [10.1111/sms.12608](https://doi.org/10.1111/sms.12608).
   - Design: cross-sectional, Danish Health Examination Survey, n=16,025. Verified claim: greater sitting time was inversely associated with cardiorespiratory fitness, most strongly in less-active people; no association among the highly active. Observational (association, not causation). Authors/journal/n confirmed.

35. **Lifestyle / cold-water immersion.** Malta ES, Dutra YM, Broatch JR, Bishop DJ, Zagatto AM. "The Effects of Regular Cold-Water Immersion Use on Training-Induced Changes in Strength and Endurance Performance: A Systematic Review with Meta-Analysis." *Sports Medicine.* 2021;51(1):161-174. DOI [10.1007/s40279-020-01362-0](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-020-01362-0).
   - Design: systematic review + meta-analysis, 8 controlled studies, repeated CWI (≤15 °C) vs passive recovery during training programs. Verified claim: regular post-training CWI moderately blunts strength/power adaptations (overall SMD -0.60, p<0.0001) but does NOT compromise endurance/aerobic performance gains; authors conclude CWI can remain in an endurance athlete's recovery. Note: some single studies suggest possible VO2max attenuation, but this meta found endurance adaptations preserved; row framed as neutral for VO2max. Authors/journal/pages confirmed.

### Batch 8 (added 2026-06-02) — Wearable VO2 max estimation accuracy

36. **Wearable accuracy / systematic review.** Železnik Mežan L. "Accuracy of wearables for determining the maximal oxygen uptake and lactate threshold: a qualitative systematic review." *Frontiers in Sports and Active Living.* 2025. PMCID PMC12748164. DOI [10.3389/fspor.2025.1707991](https://doi.org/10.3389/fspor.2025.1707991).
   - Design: PRISMA systematic review (QUADAS-2), 13 of 252 records included; most studies were Garmin smartwatches with a compatible chest belt and the Firstbeat algorithm. Verified claim: in 7 of 13 studies wearables were valid/acceptable for VO2max vs gold standard; valid in untrained/recreational/team-sport populations, questionable in elite endurance athletes. Reported device figures include Garmin Fenix 6 MAPE ~6.85% (CCC 0.70) and Forerunner 920XT MAPE ~7.3%. Author/journal/DOI confirmed.

37. **Wearable accuracy / Apple Watch.** Lambe R, O'Grady B, Baldwin M, Doherty C. "Investigating the accuracy of Apple Watch VO2 max measurements: A validation study." *PLOS ONE.* 2025;20(5):e0323741. PMCID PMC12080799. DOI [10.1371/journal.pone.0323741](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0323741).
   - Design: validation study, n=30; 5-10 days of watch wear to generate an estimate, then maximal treadmill test (modified Åstrand), Bland-Altman + MAPE/MAE. Verified figures: Apple Watch underestimated VO2max by 6.07 mL/kg/min (95% CI 3.77-8.38); MAPE 13.31%; MAE 6.92 mL/kg/min. Authors conclude estimates need refinement before clinical use. Authors/journal/figures confirmed.

38. **Wearable accuracy / Fitbit.** Freeberg KA, Baughman BR, Vickey T, Sullivan JA, Sawyer BJ. "Assessing the ability of the Fitbit Charge 2 to accurately predict VO2max." *mHealth.* 2019;5:39. PMID [31620466](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31620466/).
   - Design: validation study, n=30 (17 M, 13 F, ages 18-35); 7 days of wear, treadmill VO2max with verification phase. Verified claim: Fitbit Charge 2 cardio fitness score is a consistent/unbiased estimate but significantly overestimates absolute VO2max vs the gold standard (CFS 52.5 vs measured 49.9 mL/kg/min, p=0.03); a non-exercise equation was similarly accurate. Authors/journal confirmed.
   - Vendor/non-peer-reviewed sources referenced in the section (labeled as such, not independently verified): Firstbeat/Garmin VO2max white paper (claims ~95% accuracy, MAPE ~5%); WHOOP VO2max methodology and accuracy claims (within 3.3-3.7 mL/kg/min); Oura Cardio Capacity (in-app 6-minute walk test, developed with KULTU/UCLA); Samsung Galaxy Watch VO2max (company-associated University of Michigan testing, ~82% correlation); Apple/Fitbit/Google support docs for estimation methods.

39. **Wearable accuracy / meta-analysis (key).** Molina-García P, Notbohm HL, Schumann M, Argent R, Hetherington-Rauth M, Stang J, Bloch W, Cheng S, Ekelund U, Sardinha LB, Caulfield B, Brønd JC, Grøntved A, Ortega FB. "Validity of Estimating the Maximal Oxygen Consumption by Consumer Wearables: A Systematic Review with Meta-analysis and Expert Statement of the INTERLIVE Network." *Sports Medicine.* 2022;52(7):1577-1597. PMCID [PMC9213394](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9213394/). DOI [10.1007/s40279-021-01639-y](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-021-01639-y).
   - Design: systematic review + meta-analysis + expert statement. Verified figures: exercise-based wearable algorithms had near-nil mean bias vs lab (−0.09 mL/kg/min); resting-based algorithms overestimated VO2max (bias +2.17 mL/kg/min, limits of agreement −13.07 to +17.41). Individual-level error is large for all methods. The most authoritative organizing source for this section. Authors/journal/DOI confirmed.

40. **Wearable accuracy / Polar.** Neudorfer M, Ötzlinger L, Kumar D, Niebauer J, Smeddinck JD, Sareban M, Treff G. "Validation of the Polar Fitness Test for Estimation of Maximal Oxygen Consumption at Rest in Medically Supervised Exercise Training: Comparison with CPET and the 6-Minute Walk Test." *Sensors.* 2025;25(18):5649. PMCID [PMC12473405](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12473405/). DOI [10.3390/s25185649](https://doi.org/10.3390/s25185649).
   - Design: validation study, n=24 (mean age 57.4, 7 on HR-limiting meds), Polar Fitness Test (resting) vs CPET and 6MWT. Verified figures: PFT MAPE 13.7%, ICC 0.743, mean bias −1.0 mL/min/kg, LoA ±11.4 mL/min/kg vs CPET; the Porcari 6MWT equation performed similarly. Authors/journal/figures confirmed.

41. **Wearable accuracy / Apple Watch Series 7.** Caserman P, Yum S, Göbel S, Reif A, Matura S. "Assessing the Accuracy of Smartwatch-Based Estimation of Maximum Oxygen Uptake Using the Apple Watch Series 7: Validation Study." *JMIR Biomedical Engineering.* 2024;9:e59459. PMID [39083800](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39083800/). DOI [10.2196/59459](https://doi.org/10.2196/59459).
   - Design: validation study, n=19 (ages 18-63), cycle-ergometer graded test with metabolic gas analyzer. Verified figures: MAPE 15.79%; Apple Watch significantly underestimated VO2max (predicted 41.37 vs measured 45.88 mL/kg/min). Consistent with Lambe 2025 (Apple underestimates). Authors/journal/figures confirmed.

42. **Wearable accuracy / Garmin (independent).** Carrier B, Marten Chaves S, Navalta JW. "Validation of Aerobic Capacity (VO2max) and Pulse Oximetry in Wearable Technology." *Sensors.* 2025;25(1):275. PMCID [PMC11723475](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11723475/). DOI [10.3390/s25010275](https://doi.org/10.3390/s25010275).
   - Design: independent validation, Garmin fēnix 6 vs metabolic system (graded exercise test + outdoor run); VO2max n=19, pulse-ox n=22. Verified figures: VO2max MAPE 7.05% (30 s avg), Lin's CCC 0.73 — accurate; but blood-oxygen (SpO2) failed to be accurate under any condition. Independent (University of Nevada, Las Vegas), not vendor-funded. Authors/journal/figures confirmed.

43. **Wearable accuracy / Garmin highly-trained (independent).** Engel FA, et al. "Validity of V̇O2max estimates from the Forerunner 245 smartwatch in highly vs. moderately trained endurance athletes." *European Journal of Applied Physiology.* 2026;126:591-603 (online Aug 2025). PMID [40770433](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40770433/). DOI [10.1007/s00421-025-05923-x](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-025-05923-x).
   - Design: independent validation, n=35 endurance athletes (VO2max 60.1±8.2), Garmin Forerunner 245 vs treadmill respiratory gas analysis, two 15-min outdoor runs >70% HRmax. Verified figures: overall MAPE 6.7%; moderately trained MAPE 2.8-4.1%; highly trained underestimated (MAPE 9.4-10.4%, mean diff −4.73/−4.05 mL/kg/min). Confirms exercise-based estimates are valid in moderately trained but degrade in highly trained. Authors (Engel et al.)/journal/figures confirmed.

44. **Wearable accuracy / Samsung (heart rate, not VO2 max).** Inoue A, Soares J, Antunes-Santos F, Ferreira A, Gonçalves A, Alcântara J, dos Santos M. "Heart Rate Estimation Using the Galaxy Watch During Maximal Cardiopulmonary Exercise Testing: Cross-Sectional Validation Study." *JMIR Cardio.* 2026;10:e81917. PMCID [PMC13086260](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13086260/). DOI [10.2196/81917](https://doi.org/10.2196/81917).
   - Design: independent validation, n=55, Galaxy Watch 6 vs Polar H10 during a maximal treadmill CPET. Verified scope: this study validates **heart rate** (good agreement, ICC; error rises modestly near max), NOT VO2 max. Included only to substantiate that the independent evidence for Samsung covers HR, not VO2 max. The often-cited "Galaxy Watch VO2 max ±4.7 mL/kg/min (Frontiers, n=84)" figure could not be verified against any indexed source and is excluded as likely fabricated.

### Batch 9 (added 2026-06-02) — Input-signal accuracy (heart rate, GPS)

These support the "What Each Device Measures" tables. They validate the *inputs* to VO2 max (HR, GPS), not VO2 max itself.

45. **Wrist HR vs energy expenditure.** Shcherbina A, Mattsson CM, Waggott D, Salisbury H, Christle JW, Hastie T, Wheeler MT, Ashley EA. "Accuracy in Wrist-Worn, Sensor-Based Measurements of Heart Rate and Energy Expenditure in a Diverse Cohort." *Journal of Personalized Medicine.* 2017;7(2):3. PMCID [PMC5491979](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5491979/). DOI [10.3390/jpm7020003](https://doi.org/10.3390/jpm7020003).
   - Design: Stanford, n=60, 7 wrist devices vs ECG + indirect calorimetry, treadmill/cycle. Verified: most devices measured HR within ~5% (6 of 7 acceptable), but none measured energy expenditure acceptably (most-accurate device off ~27%, worst ~93%). Keystone showing wrist HR is decent but derived metrics are not.

46. **Chest-strap HR validity.** Gilgen-Ammann R, Schweizer T, Wyss T. "RR interval signal quality of a heart rate monitor and an ECG Holter at rest and during exercise." *European Journal of Applied Physiology.* 2019;119(7):1525-1532. DOI [10.1007/s00421-019-04142-5](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-019-04142-5).
   - Design: n=10, Polar H10 chest strap vs medilog ECG Holter across five rest-to-high-intensity activities. Verified: chest-strap RR/HR signal quality is effectively ECG-grade (>99% of beats correctly detected) at rest and during exercise. Establishes the chest strap as the best HR input.

47. **GPS distance/pace accuracy.** "Accuracy of Distance Recordings in Eight Positioning-Enabled Sport Watches: Instrument Validation Study." *JMIR mHealth and uHealth.* 2020;8(6):e17118. PMID [32396865](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32396865/). DOI [10.2196/17118](https://doi.org/10.2196/17118).
   - Design: 8 sport watches (Apple, Coros, Garmin, Polar, Suunto) across urban/forest/track while walking/running/cycling. Verified figures: MAPE 3.2-6.1%; only Polar's receivers <5% overall; distances underestimated and least accurate in urban/forest. Establishes GPS pace error as a secondary input limitation.
   - Cross-referenced (already verified in file #06 Wearable Accuracy, not re-listed here): Dial MB et al. 2025 (*Physiological Reports*, independent — active HR r=0.99 chest / 0.80 Apple / 0.52 Garmin; resting-HR CCC Oura 0.98, WHOOP 0.91, Polar 0.86); Miller DJ et al. 2022 (*Sensors* — 6-device HR/HRV validation).

### Batch 10 (added 2026-06-02) — Why it matters

48. **Clinical / mortality.** Mandsager K, Harb S, Cremer P, Phelan D, Nissen SE, Jaber W. "Association of Cardiorespiratory Fitness With Long-term Mortality Among Adults Undergoing Exercise Treadmill Testing." *JAMA Network Open.* 2018;1(6):e183605. PMID [30646252](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30646252/). PMCID [PMC6324439](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6324439/). DOI [10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2018.3605](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2018.3605).
   - Design: retrospective cohort, n=122,007 (Cleveland Clinic), mean follow-up 8.4 yr, 13,637 deaths. Verified findings: cardiorespiratory fitness was inversely associated with all-cause mortality with no observed upper limit of benefit; lowest-fitness group had ~5x the adjusted mortality risk vs elite (HR ~5.0); the paper notes poor fitness carried risk comparable to or greater than coronary disease, diabetes, and smoking. The motivational anchor for the whole topic. Authors/journal/figures confirmed.
